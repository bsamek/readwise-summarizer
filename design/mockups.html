Design Overview

Three concepts for the TLDR summary inbox web app. Click the tabs above to see interactive mockups of each.

Concept A: Card Stream

  • Layout: Single-column feed of full summary cards, like Substack's reader or Pocket
  • Strengths: Summaries are short (3–5 paragraphs), so each card shows the full text — no click needed to read. Actions sit below each summary. Tabs switch between Inbox / Read Later / Archive.
  • Weakness: Scrolling can feel long with 10+ unread summaries. Less efficient for rapid triage.

Concept B: Two-Pane Mail Client

  • Layout: Three-panel layout: sidebar nav, item list, and reader pane — like Gmail or Outlook
  • Strengths: Familiar to email users. Sidebar navigation between Inbox / Read Later / Archive. Clicking a list item shows the summary in the reader pane without losing your place.
  • Weakness: Heavier UI for what is fundamentally a simple list of short texts. Requires wider screens; mobile needs a different layout. Overkill for 5–15 items.

Concept C: Compact Triage List

  • Layout: Dense rows with inline icon actions. Click a title to expand the summary inline. Like a minimal task manager or Readwise inbox.
  • Strengths: See many items at once — fastest for triage. Inline expand lets you read without leaving context. Icon buttons for quick actions (archive, save, listen). Works well on both desktop and mobile.
  • Weakness: Expanded summaries push the list down, so you lose sight of items below.

Recommendation: Concept C (Compact Triage)

Concept C is the best fit because the core workflow is triage — quickly deciding what to do with each summary. Here's why:

  • Matches the use case: You want to scan titles, decide whether to read now, save, or archive — not read every summary top to bottom. Dense rows let you see your whole inbox at a glance.
  • Click-to-expand is ideal for summaries: Summaries are short enough that expanding them inline works perfectly. No need for a separate reader pane. You read the summary, decide, and move on.
  • Scales down: Works on mobile without a completely different layout. The two-pane mail client (B) collapses badly on small screens.
  • Not overkill: A full mail client (B) is too heavy for what's essentially a short list. Card stream (A) is pretty but slow to triage. Concept C hits the sweet spot.
  • Icon actions are fast: One click to archive, save, or listen — no need to expand first. Power users can blow through their inbox.

The AI landscape is shifting toward smaller, specialized models

From Stratechery by Ben Thompson

Thompson argues that the AI industry is undergoing a fundamental shift away from the "bigger is better" paradigm. While frontier labs continue to train massive models, the real innovation is happening at the edge — smaller models fine-tuned for specific tasks are outperforming their larger counterparts in production.

He draws a parallel to the early days of cloud computing, where enterprises initially thought they'd need massive centralized systems but eventually moved toward microservices. The same pattern is emerging in AI: specialized 7B models running locally are replacing API calls to 400B+ parameter models for many use cases.

The piece concludes with implications for the competitive landscape. Companies that can efficiently fine-tune and deploy small models may have a durable advantage over those betting solely on scale.

Rust 1.85 brings async closures and improved diagnostics

From The Rust Blog

The latest Rust release stabilizes async closures, one of the most requested features since async/await landed. The new syntax allows closures to be async without wrapping them in async blocks, which simplifies callback-heavy code patterns common in web servers and event-driven architectures.

The compiler diagnostics have also been significantly improved. Error messages now suggest specific fixes more often, and the borrow checker explains its reasoning in more human-readable terms. The team notes that diagnostic quality is now a first-class priority alongside language features.

How we migrated 10M rows from Postgres to SQLite without downtime

From Kent C. Dodds Blog

A detailed account of migrating a production Rails application from PostgreSQL to SQLite, driven by the desire to simplify infrastructure and reduce costs. The team used a dual-write strategy during the transition period, sending all writes to both databases while gradually shifting reads to SQLite.

The biggest challenge was handling concurrent writes during migration. They built a custom reconciliation tool that compared records between both databases and resolved conflicts using timestamps. The entire migration took three weeks and resulted in a 40% reduction in hosting costs.
